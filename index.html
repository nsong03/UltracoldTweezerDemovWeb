UC Berkeley Ultracold Atomic Physics

Welcome to Ultracold!

We use neutral atoms trapped in optical cavities to explore many-body physics and quantum information. Here, you'll see how to load single atoms in an optical lattice.

We'll end up with a grid (or line) of atoms, which can be used to investigate quantum networking, Dicke Superradiance, and exotic spin systems.

Apparatus diagram from Emma Deist's Thesis

Atoms loading...

We start by turning on a MOT (Magneto-Optical Trap) to make a cold atomic gas and also turn on some optical tweezers (dots on the left). This tightly focused, high-power light lets us trap single atoms with a 50% chance in each site.

Apparatus diagram from Emma Deist's Thesis

Atoms loaded!

Our tweezers now contain a single atom, but the filling is probabilistic and changes on each run. This random distribution isn't too useful, so we start sorting.

From our 2023 'Super-radiant and Sub-radiant Cavity Scattering by Atom Arrays' paper

Sorting atoms...

To create a usable formation, we can move atoms trapped in optical tweezers around with a deeper optical trap (A brighter laser). This is usually done with an AOD (Acousto-Optic Deflector), and it gives us full control over the position of our atoms.

Atom movement in optical lattice. Red - Nodes, Blue - Antinodes.

Atoms sorted!

We now have an array of single atoms to play with. Notice how there's some leftover atoms? They'll come in handy soon, because our atoms will start disappearing. Thermal noise and random fluctuations give an average atom lifetime of half a second.

A line of atoms from Justin Gerber's Thesis

Resorting atoms...

While atoms disappear, we can-continuously image our cavity and do feedback-based resorting. This lets us correct for gaps and keep getting useful data.

Cavity photon number scaling from our 2023 'Super-radiant and Sub-radiant Cavity Scattering by Atom Arrays' paper

Shutting down...

All good things must come to an end. Eventually, enough atoms vanish that resorting becomes useless. This run blinks out, and a new one begins.
